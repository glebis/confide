Headline findings

  1. Three PII types — age 0%, medication 4%, profession 2% — are near-zero under the deterministic layers (Natasha NER + regex); only the local LLM raises their mention-recall.
  2. 31% of quasi-identifiers still survive the proposed default stack (both clients) — the attributes that, combined, re-identify a person.
  3. Direct identifiers reach 0.76 entity recall; quasi-identifiers stay lower at 0.64. Redacting direct IDs is necessary but not sufficient.
  4. The proposed RU default natasha+regex+ollama ★ reaches 85% coverage recall at local-first speed.
  5. Established baselines (Presidio, Philter) can match coverage but fall far behind on type-aware micro-F1 — and Presidio collapses on the real ai4privacy slice.
  6. One adversarial leak remains: a Latin-transliterated Russian name (Cyrillic-only NER + no name regex + LLM miss) — an argument for Latin NER where transliteration is expected.

Problem field

Psychotherapy, coaching and mentoring transcripts are among the most sensitive personal text that exists — they name people, places, employers, diagnoses, medications and ages, often in the same sentence. Yet practitioners increasingly want to run them through cloud LLMs for analysis. De-identification is the gate: strip the personally identifying information before the text leaves the clinician's machine. The hard part is that de-identification is not one tool but a stack of detectors — pattern rules, named-entity recognition, privacy filters, and a local LLM — and it is rarely clear which layer actually earns the compute it burns, or what still leaks after redaction.

Motivation

This benchmark composes detector layers by span-union over Russian and English psychotherapy transcripts, scores each combination the way published de-identification work does — recall-first, entity-level, direct vs quasi — and asks a sharper question than “how good is the tool”: what can only an LLM catch, and what survives after we redact? The aim is a reproducible, layered ablation that tells a practitioner which detectors to deploy, what the residual re-identification risk is, and whether de-identification preserves enough signal to still analyze the session.

Findings

De-identification is not one tool but a stack of detectors, and the honest question is which layer pays for the CPU it burns.

Which layer catches what

The LLM layer is what moves age, medication and profession above the deterministic baseline.

RU per-category mention recall (relaxed overlap), 1078 gold mentions: deterministic (Natasha+regex) vs. +qwen.

reading it

Structured direct IDs (email, phone, policy ID) and names/orgs/locations reach 0.9–1.0 from regex + Natasha. Dates are now caught too — a numeric-date regex rule was added after the benchmark exposed the gap.

Quasi-identifiers needing world-knowledge — a drug name, an occupation, a spelled-out age — are invisible to pattern and NER layers; the LLM is the only layer that lifts their mention-recall.

Caveat: this is mention recall. At entity level (all mentions masked), medication and profession stay at 0 even with qwen — a higher bar.

Direct vs quasi-identifiers

Direct identifiers reach 0.76 entity recall; quasi-identifiers remain lower at 0.64.

RU entity-level recall (an entity is protected only if all mentions are masked) by identifier class.

the asymmetry

Direct (name, phone, email, policy): masked at 0.76 entity recall in the default stack.

Quasi (age, profession, city, employer, medication, date): the LLM helps but the ceiling stays low — these are the attributes that, combined, re-identify a person.

Method — following the re-identification / inference-attack literature (Staab et al.; RAT-Bench; Tau-Eval). An entity survives if any one of its mentions is left unmasked.

interpretation

A local 3B qwen attacker recovered 2 of 10 tested attributes from the redacted text (e.g. the medication, because its entity-recall is 0). A weak attacker is a lower bound — the inference-attack literature reports much higher re-identification rates for frontier models.

Implication: redacting direct identifiers is table stakes; quasi-identifier survival is a useful gate to check before sending a session to cloud analysis.

Method — top-k inference attack with a fixed, declared budget (qwen-3B, temp 0.4, top-3 guesses/attribute, redacted text only) + downstream task preservation (re-run cognitive-distortion extraction on redacted vs. original). Aligned with Staab et al. / RAT-Bench (privacy) and Tau-Eval (task-sensitive utility).

the tension, resolved

The same masking that lowers attacker success can erase clinical content. Here it does not: the default stack keeps ~91% of distortion signal and 99.5% of non-PII text while a weak attacker recovers nothing top-3.

Caveat: this attacker is a lower bound — quasi-identifiers still survive in text (medication entity-recall is 0), so a frontier attacker would score higher. Residual risk stays MEDIUM for client B.

Stack vs baselines

Off-the-shelf de-identifiers can match the stack on coverage but fall far behind on type-aware micro-F1 — and Presidio collapses on the real ai4privacy slice.

EN-synth: coverage F2 (recall-weighted, headline) vs type-aware micro-F1 for the CONFIDE ★ stack and the established baselines.

EN-real (ai4privacy): same two metrics. Presidio's coverage drops sharply on real-world markup/ID formats.

reading it

Coverage F2 (orange) asks only “did we mask the span at all”. Type micro-F1 (green) demands the right label too — what a redaction policy actually needs.

On EN-synth, Presidio edges the stack on coverage F2 (a broad DATE_TIME recognizer) but its type-F1 is far lower; Philter is high-coverage yet emits almost everything as untyped OTHER, so its type-F1 is unusable.

On EN-real, Presidio collapses on coverage — generic NER + structured recognizers miss the bespoke ID/markup formats the stack catches.

Takeaway: a generic system is not a therapy-tuned one; the only coverage a baseline adds is relative/colloquial dates.

Limitations

  • Small N. Each dataset is small enough that one missed entity moves recall several points — treat per-type numbers as directional, not definitive.
  • Synthetic Russian data. The RU series is fully fictional, hand-built from two answer-key PII inventories; it is a planted-signal recovery eval, not independently double-annotated gold.
  • Mention vs entity. Headline numbers are mention-level recall; the stricter entity-level bar (all mentions masked) is lower, especially for recurring quasi-identifiers.
  • Weak attacker. The re-identification attacker is a local 3B model — a lower bound. Frontier models infer far more from the same redacted text.
  • Out of scope by design. Spelled-out digit strings are not targeted by the regex layer; the Latin-transliterated-name leak shows the Cyrillic-only NER gap.
  • Not a compliance certificate. Benchmark success is not HIPAA or GDPR compliance; the type mapping is illustrative, and any real session data needs consent + ethics review.

Available data

Four datasets ship with the benchmark. RU-synth — a fully synthetic, fictional Russian therapy series (client-a + client-b, 10 sessions), gold located programmatically from curated Cyrillic-morphology-aware surface patterns and hand-verified, every mention carrying an entity_id for entity-level scoring. RU-adv — an adversarial probe (transliteration, code-switching, structured IDs, handles). EN-synth — a curated synthetic English slice. EN-real — a real ai4privacy/pii-masking-300k validation slice. Splits are person-disjoint (client-a = dev, client-b = test) so no profile leaks across splits. Per-dataset scores live in results/*-bench-results.json; reconstruction and privacy-utility results in their own JSONs. Full datasheet and data statement: docs/DATASHEET.md; reproduction steps in docs/REPRODUCIBILITY.md; the canonical write-up in docs/BENCHMARK.md. All content is synthetic/fictional, released for research and teaching — not real patient data.

Methodology

Methodology

Each detector runs once per dataset; combinations are span-unions of cached spans, interval-merged to the deployed redaction mask before scoring. This report headlines coverage recall (relaxed overlap) — the privacy-critical number — and recall-weighted F2 + precision sit in the leaderboard table. Type-aware micro/macro-F1 (i2b2) and entity-level recall (TAB; all mentions masked) are also reported. Numbers are mention-level unless marked entity-level. Gold for RU is located from the two answer-key PII inventories and hand-verified (a planted-signal recovery eval). Synthetic data — no real patients. Small N: treat per-type numbers as directional.
